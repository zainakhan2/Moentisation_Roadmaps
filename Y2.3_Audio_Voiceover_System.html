Mic technique — get this right and post-processing becomes easy:
RuleDetailsWhy
Distance6–8 inches (15–20 cm) from mouth to micToo close = bass-heavy, popping. Too far = thin, echoey, noisy.
AngleSpeak slightly off-axis (15–30° to the side of the mic, not directly into it)Reduces plosives and breath noise without losing clarity.
Gain levelSet input gain so your voice peaks between -12 dB and -6 dB in Audacity's meterLeaves headroom for loud moments. Prevents clipping (distortion). You can always boost volume in post — you can never fix clipping.
Room toneBefore every recording session, record 5–10 seconds of complete silenceThis "noise profile" is used by Audacity's Noise Reduction to identify and remove background noise. Essential step.
ConsistencySame mic position, same room, same time of day for every recordingEnsures all your videos sound consistent. Listeners notice when audio quality changes between videos.
MASTERING

The 7-Step Audacity Mastering Chain — Do This Every Time

This is the exact processing chain that turns a raw mic recording into professional-sounding narration. Follow these 7 steps IN ORDER — the sequence matters.

Step 1: Noise Reduction (remove background hum/hiss)

ActionHow
Get noise profileSelect 3–5 seconds of the silent "room tone" you recorded at the start → Effect → Noise Reduction → "Get Noise Profile"
Apply reductionSelect All (Ctrl+A) → Effect → Noise Reduction → Settings: Noise Reduction: 12 dB, Sensitivity: 6, Frequency Smoothing: 3 → Preview → OK
If voice sounds "underwater" or robotic after applying, reduce the dB value to 8–10. Less is more — a tiny hiss is better than a robotic voice.

Step 2: Normalize (bring to standard volume)

ActionHow
NormalizeSelect All (Ctrl+A) → Effect → Volume and Compression → Normalize → Peak amplitude: -1.0 dB → Check "Remove DC offset" → OK
This brings your audio to a consistent baseline volume. The loudest peak will sit at -1 dB, leaving headroom.

Step 3: EQ — High-Pass Filter (remove low rumble)

ActionHow
Cut low frequenciesSelect All → Effect → Filter Curve EQ → Add a point at 80 Hz → drag everything below 80 Hz down to -24 dB (creates a steep roll-off) → OK
Removes low-frequency rumble: traffic, AC hum, mic handling noise. Frequencies below 80 Hz don't contain useful voice information for narration.

Step 4: EQ — Presence Boost (add clarity)

ActionHow
Boost presenceSelect All → Effect → Filter Curve EQ → Add a gentle boost of +2 to +4 dB around 3–5 kHz → Preview → adjust to taste → OK
This is the "intelligibility" range of the human voice. A subtle boost here makes your narration cut through background music clearly. Don't overdo it — +2 dB is often enough. If your voice sounds harsh or sibilant, reduce or skip this step.

Step 5: Compression (even out volume)

ActionHow
Compress dynamicsSelect All → Effect → Volume and Compression → Compressor → Settings: Threshold: -18 dB, Ratio: 3:1, Attack: 0.10 sec, Release: 1.0 sec → Check "Make-up gain for 0 dB after compressing" → OK
Compression makes quiet words louder and loud words quieter — giving your voice a consistent, professional volume throughout. This is the single most impactful processing step for narration quality.

Step 6: Second Normalize (final volume)

ActionHow
Final normalizeSelect All → Effect → Volume and Compression → Normalize → Peak amplitude: -1.0 dB → OK
Compression may have changed the overall volume. This second normalize ensures consistent peak levels for the final export.

Step 7: Limiter (prevent clipping)

ActionHow
Apply hard limiterSelect All → Effect → Volume and Compression → Limiter → Type: Hard Limit, dB limit: -0.5 dB → OK
Safety net — prevents any accidental peaks from clipping. Ensures your audio never distorts even on sensitive playback systems.
Time for full chain: 5–7 minutes. Once you've done this 3–4 times, it becomes muscle memory. You can save each effect's settings as a preset in Audacity (Manage → Save Preset) so you apply them with one click. The entire chain can also be applied as a Macro (Tools → Macros → New) for one-click batch processing.
Audacity Macro setup (optional but saves 3 min/video): Tools → Macros → New → name it "TFF Voiceover" → Insert steps in order: Noise Reduction, Normalize, Filter Curve EQ (high-pass), Filter Curve EQ (presence), Compressor, Normalize, Limiter. Save. Now for every recording: select all → Tools → Apply Macro → TFF Voiceover. One click, done.

PERFORMANCE

Narration Performance Guide — How to Sound Like a Documentary Host

Great audio quality means nothing if the delivery is flat. Here's how to perform your narration like a professional documentary narrator.
TechniqueWhat to DoWhen to Use
Documentary paceSpeak at ~130–140 words per minute. This is noticeably slower than conversational speech (~170 wpm). Pause 1–2 seconds between paragraphs. Let sentences breathe.Your default pace for all narration. Practice by timing yourself reading 140 words and aiming for exactly 60 seconds.
Emphasis through silenceBefore a key fact or number, pause for 1 full second. Then deliver the line. Then pause again. "...(pause)...87,000 soldiers...(pause)...never came home."Statistics, shocking facts, emotional reveals. Silence is the most powerful tool in audio.
Volume dynamicsDon't narrate at one volume. Get louder during tension and climax. Drop to near-whisper for emotional or reflective moments. Let your voice reflect the emotion of the content.Map to your emotion labels from Y2.1: SHOCK = measured/deliberate, TENSION = rising energy, SADNESS = quiet/slow, REFLECTION = calm/steady.
Emphasis wordsIn your script, underline or bold the ONE word in each sentence that carries the meaning. Hit that word slightly harder in delivery. "87,000 soldiers died fighting for an empire that forgot their names."Every sentence. Practice reading with emphasis on different words to hear how meaning changes.
Breath controlTake a breath BEFORE you start speaking, not mid-sentence. If a sentence is too long to say in one breath, shorten it in the script. Gasping mid-sentence sounds amateur.Always. Edit your script to eliminate sentences longer than ~20 words — they're too long for comfortable single-breath delivery.
Re-recordingIf you flub a line, pause 2 seconds, then re-read the ENTIRE sentence from the beginning. Don't try to patch mid-word. In editing, you'll cut the flubbed version and keep the clean one.Whenever you make a mistake. This is faster than trying to edit a partial re-read to match.
Warm upBefore every recording session, read one paragraph of your script out loud at full volume 2–3 times. This warms your vocal cords and sets the pace/tone for the session.First 2 minutes of every recording session. Cold reads sound noticeably worse than warmed-up reads.
The accent advantage: Your South Asian accent is a FEATURE, not a bug. When narrating stories about Indian soldiers in WW2, a voice that sounds like it belongs to the culture being discussed is inherently more authentic than a generic American or British narrator. Focus on clarity and pacing — never try to change or hide your accent. The audience will find it distinctive and trustworthy.
MUSIC

Background Music Strategy

RuleDetails
Volume levelBackground music sits at 10–15% of voiceover volume in CapCut. The viewer should never consciously notice the music — it should create a mood without competing with your voice. If someone says "nice music" they heard it too loudly.
Music duckingIn CapCut, reduce music volume during narration-heavy sections. Slightly raise music volume during visual-only moments (pauses between narration, black screens, map transitions). This creates a natural breathing rhythm.
One track per videoUse ONE continuous music track per video. Changing tracks mid-video is jarring and amateurish. Choose a track that works for the overall mood, then use volume changes to modulate intensity.
Mood matchingYouTube Audio Library search terms for The Forgotten Front: "dark ambient", "cinematic suspense", "somber piano", "tension documentary", "military drums", "melancholy strings".
Music at start/endBring music in BEFORE voiceover starts (first 2–3 seconds). Let music continue for 2–3 seconds AFTER your sign-off ends. This creates professional "breathing room" around your narration.

QC

Audio Quality Checklist (Before Exporting)

CheckWhat to Verify
☐ Noise floorPlay a silent section — is there audible hiss or hum? If yes, re-run noise reduction (lower dB value).
☐ ClippingLook at the waveform — are there any flat-topped peaks? If yes, redo with lower input gain and re-record.
☐ Consistent volumeSkip to 3 random points in the recording. Does your voice sound the same volume at each? Compression should handle this.
☐ ClarityPut on headphones. Can you clearly understand every word? No mumbling, no buried consonants?
☐ PlosivesListen for harsh "P" and "B" pops. If present, re-record those sentences with the pop filter angled differently.
☐ Mouth soundsListen for excessive lip smacking, clicks, or saliva sounds between words. Hydrate before recording. Edit out prominent clicks.
☐ Room echoDoes your voice sound like it's in a bathroom? If yes, add soft materials around your recording space (blankets, pillows, clothes).
☐ Breath removalLoud breaths between sentences should be manually reduced (select the breath → Effect → Amplify → -15 dB). Don't delete breaths entirely — some are natural. Just quiet them.
☐ PacingPlay back at 1× speed. Does it sound too rushed or too slow? Target ~140 wpm documentary pace.
☐ Export formatWAV for CapCut editing. MP3 (320kbps) for backup.
FIXES

Common Audio Problems & Fixes

ProblemCauseFix
Voice sounds thin/tinnyToo far from mic, or mic gain too lowMove closer to mic (6 inches). Increase input gain. Add slight EQ boost at 120–200 Hz for warmth.
Voice sounds boomy/muddyToo close to mic (proximity effect), or room resonanceMove back to 8 inches. Apply stronger high-pass filter (cut below 100 Hz instead of 80 Hz). Reduce EQ at 200–400 Hz by -2 to -3 dB.
Background hiss/humNoisy environment, cheap mic, electrical interferenceRe-run Noise Reduction. Record at night. Move mic away from laptop fans and power supplies. Use a USB extension cable to separate mic from laptop.
Voice sounds robotic after noise reductionNoise Reduction dB value too highRedo with lower settings: try 6–8 dB instead of 12 dB. Accept a tiny hiss — it's better than robotic artifacts.
Volume jumps between sentencesInconsistent mic distance during recordingUse a mic stand (or stack of books) to fix mic position. Don't hold mic in hand. Apply compression with lower threshold (-20 dB).
Echo/reverbHard surfaces in recording roomRecord in a closet with clothes. Drape a blanket over yourself and the mic ("blanket fort" technique — professional voice actors do this). Add soft furnishings around recording spot.
Harsh "S" sounds (sibilance)Mic picking up sibilance, or too much high-end EQReduce presence boost (Step 4 of mastering chain). Try angling mic slightly more off-axis. If persistent, apply EQ cut of -2 dB at 6–8 kHz.
SESSION

Recording Session SOP — 20 Minutes Per Video

MinuteAction
0:00Setup: Position mic on stand. Open Audacity. Set recording to Mono, 44.1 kHz, 16-bit. Check input gain peaks at -12 to -6 dB.
0:02Room tone: Hit Record. Sit silently for 5 seconds. This captures your noise profile for Step 1 of mastering chain.
0:03Warm-up: Read the first paragraph of your script out loud 2× at full volume. This isn't recorded for use — it's vocal warm-up.
0:05Full read: Start reading the script. Documentary pace (~140 wpm). When you flub a line: pause 2 seconds, then re-read the entire sentence. Don't stop recording between sections — edit later.
0:17Done reading. Stop recording. Total narration time for a 15-min video (~2,100 words at 140 wpm) = about 15 minutes of reading + 2–3 minutes for re-reads = ~17 minutes of raw audio.
0:17Quick edit: Delete flubbed takes (keep clean re-reads). Remove long silences. Don't process yet — save the raw file as backup.
0:20Mastering chain: Apply the 7-step chain. Export as WAV. Done. File goes to TheForgottenFront/Audio/TFF-[number]-voiceover.wav
Total recording + processing time: 20 minutes per video. This matches the Y1.4 production pipeline estimate. By Video 5, you'll do this in 15 minutes. By Video 10, the mastering chain is automatic (Audacity Macro) and the whole session takes 12 minutes.
