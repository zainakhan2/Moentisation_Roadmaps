PHILOSOPHY

The #1 Rule of Documentary YouTube Scripts

"The only thing that keeps someone watching is not knowing how something is going to turn out — even if they already know how it turned out." — Ken Burns. Your script must create uncertainty about events whose outcomes are already historical fact. The viewer knows India won at Imphal. But they DON'T know the specific moment everything nearly fell apart — until you show them.
What separates a great military history script from a Wikipedia read-aloud:
Wikipedia-Style (BAD)Documentary-Style (GOOD)
"The Battle of Imphal took place from March to July 1944 in northeast India. It was fought between British and Indian forces against the Japanese.""By April 1944, 150,000 Japanese soldiers were closing in on a city most of the world had never heard of. If Imphal fell, Japan would pour into India — and the war would take a completely different turn. Between the Japanese army and that future stood a force the British Empire had never fully trusted: Indian soldiers."
Lists facts chronologicallyCreates tension about a known outcome
Tells the viewer what happenedMakes the viewer FEEL what it was like
Passive voice, neutral toneActive voice, dramatic pacing, emotional stakes
STRUCTURES

3 Story Structures for The Forgotten Front

Every video uses one of these three structures. Pick based on the type of story.

Structure A: "The Arc" — Classic Documentary (60% of your videos)

Use for: Battle stories, forgotten soldiers, military campaigns. Any story with a clear protagonist, conflict, and outcome.
SectionDuration (15 min video)PurposeWriting Technique
1. COLD OPEN0:00–0:30Shock, intrigue, or emotion. The most dramatic single moment from the entire story, ripped out of context."In medias res" — start in the middle of the action. Drop the viewer into the most intense moment without explanation. They MUST keep watching to understand what they just heard.
2. TITLE CARD0:30–0:33Brand stamp. 3 seconds max."THE FORGOTTEN FRONT" logo flash with dramatic sting.
3. CONTEXT0:33–3:00Set the scene. Who, where, when. Establish the stakes — what's at risk if things go wrong?Zoom out from the cold open. Give the viewer the knowledge they need to care. Answer: "Why should I care about this?" within 90 seconds.
4. RISING ACTION3:00–8:00The story unfolds. Key decisions, turning points, complications. Things get worse before they get better.Use "micro-loops" — end each 60–90 second segment with a question or tension point that makes the viewer need to hear the next segment. Never resolve tension immediately.
5. CLIMAX8:00–11:00The most dramatic moment. The battle, the decision, the revelation.Slow your narration pace. Add dramatic pauses. Let the music build. This is where your voice performance matters most.
6. AFTERMATH11:00–13:30What happened next? Consequences. Legacy. How this event changed history.Shift from dramatic to reflective. Lower your voice. This is the "meaning" section — why this story matters.
7. "THE FORGOTTEN" MOMENT13:30–14:30The emotional punch. Why this story was forgotten. Who was erased from history. The injustice.This is YOUR brand signature. Every video has a moment where you explicitly address WHY this story isn't taught. This creates the emotional payoff that makes viewers subscribe.
8. SIGN-OFF + CTA14:30–15:00"This was The Forgotten Front." Subscribe. End screen.Consistent sign-off builds brand recognition. Never skip it.

Structure B: "The Question" — Strategic Analysis (25% of your videos)

Use for: "Why did they lose?" videos, strategic analysis, decision breakdowns. Videos where the ANSWER is the hook.
SectionDurationPurpose
1. THE QUESTION0:00–0:45State the central question dramatically. "80,000 soldiers surrendered in a single day. The largest capitulation in British military history. How could this possibly happen?"
2. THE OBVIOUS ANSWER0:45–2:30Present the answer most people would guess. "You might think it was simply overwhelming Japanese force..." Then reveal why that answer is WRONG or incomplete.
3. THE REAL STORY2:30–10:00Unpack the real reasons: bad decisions, hubris, logistics failures, political context. Each reason is a mini-arc within the larger story.
4. THE ANSWER10:00–13:00Synthesize everything into a clear, memorable conclusion. "Singapore fell because of three fatal assumptions that the British made..."
5. THE LESSON + SIGN-OFF13:00–15:00What can we learn from this? Why does it matter today? Sign-off.

Structure C: "The Dossier" — Espionage & Intelligence (15% of your videos)

Use for: Spy stories, intelligence operations, classified missions, dark chapters. Videos with a mystery or reveal at the center.
SectionDurationPurpose
1. THE FILE0:00–0:30Open with the mystery. "In 1943, a dead man washed up on a Spanish beach carrying military documents. He didn't exist. And the documents were lies. But they would change the outcome of the entire war."
2. THE SETUP0:30–3:00The problem that needed solving. What was the intelligence challenge?
3. THE OPERATION3:00–9:00How the operation was designed and executed. The people, the plan, the risks. Build suspense — will it work?
4. THE MOMENT OF TRUTH9:00–11:00Did it work? What almost went wrong? The tension peak.
5. DECLASSIFIED11:00–14:00The aftermath. When the truth came out. What we learned. The people behind the operation.
6. SIGN-OFF14:00–15:00"This was The Forgotten Front." CTA.
EMOTION

Emotion Mapping — The Hidden Layer of Great Scripts

Before you finalize any script, label each section with the emotion it should evoke. Varying emotions is what keeps viewers watching — not just facts.
EmotionWhere to UseHow to Create ItVoice Technique
SHOCKCold open, revealsLead with the most surprising fact. Counterintuitive numbers. "2.5 million soldiers. Zero monuments."Drop your voice low. Say it slowly. Pause after the key number.
CURIOSITYAfter every section breakAsk a question. Tease what's coming. "But what nobody knew was that the plan had a fatal flaw."Slightly raise your pitch. Speed up slightly. Energy in your voice.
TENSIONRising action, before climaxShort sentences. Countdown language. "The Japanese were 30 miles away. Then 20. Then 10."Speed up gradually. Shorter pauses. Let the urgency build.
RELIEFAfter climax resolutionThe moment things turn. "Against all odds, the line held."Exhale slightly. Slow down. Let the listener breathe.
SADNESS"The Forgotten" momentHuman details. Names. Ages. "Private Ram Singh was 19 years old. His family never learned where he was buried."Quiet. Very slow. Almost a whisper. Let silence do the work.
REFLECTIONAftermath, sign-offZoom out to the bigger picture. What does this mean? Why does it matter?Calm, steady. Authoritative but not dramatic. Let the weight sit.
The emotion sequence for a perfect 15-min video: SHOCK → CURIOSITY → build TENSION → peak TENSION → RELIEF → deeper CURIOSITY → TENSION again → CLIMAX → SADNESS → REFLECTION → SIGN-OFF. The audience should feel at least 4 different emotions during a single video. If your script produces only one emotion (usually just "informative"), it's a Wikipedia read-aloud, not a documentary.

REWRITE

The 40–60% Rewrite Rule — What to Change and What to Keep

AI gives you the skeleton. YOU give it a soul. Here's exactly what to change and what to keep from Claude's draft:
Keep From AI DraftRewrite in Your Voice
Factual accuracy (dates, names, numbers)The cold open hook — rewrite to match YOUR dramatic instinct
Chronological structureAll transitional sentences ("But what nobody knew was..." — make these YOUR style)
Research depth and obscure details"The Forgotten" moment — this must feel personally felt, not AI-generated
Visual cue suggestionsAny humor, irony, or editorial commentary
Basic narrative arcThe sign-off and CTA — must be consistently YOUR words
The test: Read the final script out loud. If any sentence feels like "AI wrote this," rewrite it. If you can't imagine yourself naturally SAYING a sentence, it doesn't belong in your script. Your narration should sound like a knowledgeable person telling a story to a friend — not like a textbook being read aloud.
RETENTION

Retention Techniques — Keeping Viewers to the End

Average View Duration (AVD) is the #1 metric YouTube uses to decide whether to recommend your video. These techniques keep viewers watching.
TechniqueWhat It IsScript ExampleWhen to Use
Open LoopsTease something coming later. Don't resolve it yet."We'll get to what happened when the message was decoded — but first, you need to understand why anyone would attempt this mission in the first place."Every 2–3 minutes. Plant a question, answer it 60–90 seconds later.
Pattern InterruptsBreak the expected flow. Surprise the viewer."Now, you'd think the British would have seen this coming. They had every piece of intelligence they needed. And they ignored ALL of it."When factual density starts to feel monotonous. Wake the viewer up.
Escalating StakesMake the situation progressively worse before the resolution."The first battalion fell. Then the second. By nightfall, the entire eastern perimeter had collapsed."Rising action section. Things must get worse before they get better.
Time PressureAdd urgency with countdown language."They had 72 hours. If reinforcements didn't arrive by dawn on April 5th, the position would be overrun."Before climax. Creates physical tension in the viewer.
"But" TransitionsEvery section transition uses "but", "however", "except" — never "and then.""The plan was perfect. But there was one thing they hadn't accounted for." NOT: "And then they executed the plan."Every section transition. "But" creates tension. "And" creates boredom.
Direct AddressSpeak directly to the viewer to re-engage."Think about that for a moment. You're 19 years old, 4,000 miles from home, fighting for an empire that doesn't consider you an equal."1–2 times per video max. Use at emotional moments for impact.
SAMPLE

Sample Script — Video #1: "The 2.5 Million Soldiers History Forgot"

This is a condensed example showing the first 3 minutes of your launch video, fully formatted with visual cues, emotion labels, and retention techniques.
COLD OPEN · 0:00–0:30
[SHOCK]
[PHOTO: Massive formation of Indian soldiers standing at attention, 1942]
"In 1939, two and a half million men volunteered to fight in a war that wasn't theirs."

[ZOOM: Slow push into faces of soldiers in the crowd]
"They fought in the deserts of North Africa. They bled in the jungles of Burma. They stormed the beaches of Italy."

[TEXT: "87,000 KILLED" — large, amber text on black]
"87,000 of them never came home."

[BLACK — 2 seconds]
"And the world... forgot."
TITLE CARD · 0:30–0:33
[TITLE: The Forgotten Front logo, dramatic sting, 3 seconds]
CONTEXT · 0:33–3:00
[CURIOSITY]
[MAP: British India, 1939 — highlighting major cities and military bases]
"When Britain declared war on Germany in September 1939, it also declared war on behalf of its entire empire — including India. Nobody asked the 400 million Indians if they wanted to fight."

[PHOTO: Indian independence leaders — Gandhi, Nehru — at a political meeting]
"India's independence leaders were furious. Gandhi called it 'a war of the imperialists.' Congress demanded self-governance as the price of cooperation."

[PHOTO: Long queue of Indian men at a military recruitment center]
"But here's what almost nobody talks about: despite the political opposition, two and a half million Indians volunteered anyway. Not conscripted. Volunteered. Making the Indian Army the largest volunteer force in human history."

[CURIOSITY → TENSION]
"The question is — why? Why would millions of men fight for an empire that ruled them as subjects?"

[TEXT: "The largest volunteer force in human history" — Oswald Bold]
"The answer is more complicated — and more human — than any textbook will tell you. And the story of what happened to these men after the war... is the part history tried to erase."
Notice what this cold open does: In 30 seconds, the viewer knows the scale (2.5 million), the sacrifice (87,000 dead), and the injustice (forgotten). They have a reason to care. The context section then opens TWO loops: (1) why did they volunteer? and (2) what happened after the war? Both loops demand answers the viewer can only get by watching. AVD will be high.
