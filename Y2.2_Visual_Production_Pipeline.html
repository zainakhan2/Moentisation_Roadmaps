Copyright safety rule (REVISED — see Y2+Y3 Gap Analysis): ONLY use images from verified-safe sources. Before downloading ANY image, check its license page:
🟢 ALWAYS SAFE: Wikimedia Commons (Public Domain / CC BY / CC0), Library of Congress, National Archives (US), Pexels, Pixabay

TREATMENT

The "Forgotten Front" Visual Treatment — Making Archival Photos Cinematic

Raw archival photos can look flat and boring. These treatments turn them into cinematic visuals that match your gritty war journalism aesthetic — all doable in CapCut for free.
TreatmentHow to Apply (CapCut)When to UseEffect
Ken Burns EffectSelect image on timeline → Keyframes → Set start position (e.g., wide) → move playhead to end → set end position (zoomed in or panned). 5–8 second duration per image.EVERY archival photo. Never show a static image — always have slow movement.Creates the illusion of motion from still photos. Makes the video feel alive, not like a slideshow.
DesaturationFilters → reduce saturation 20–40%. Or use Adjust → Saturation slider left.Color photos that feel too "modern" or bright for a war documentary aesthetic.Muted tones feel more archival and serious. Matches your dark palette.
VignetteEffects → Lens → Vignette. Or Adjust → Vignette slider. Darken edges 15–25%.Almost every image. Subtle is key — the viewer shouldn't consciously notice it.Draws the eye to the center of the image. Adds depth and focus. Creates a "looking through a lens" feel.
Grain/Noise OverlayImport a film grain overlay (search Pexels for "film grain") → place on top track → set blend mode to "Overlay" or "Screen" → reduce opacity to 10–20%.Optionally on all footage for a consistent aged documentary feel. Use sparingly.Makes everything look like it was shot on old film stock. Unifies visuals that come from different sources.
Slow Zoom to FaceKen Burns: start wide on a group photo → slowly zoom into ONE person's face over 6–8 seconds.Emotional moments. "Private Ram Singh was 19 years old..." while zooming into a young soldier.Incredibly powerful emotionally. Puts a human face on statistics. Your strongest visual technique.
Black Screen PauseAdd 1.5–2 second black frame between images at key dramatic moments.Before major reveals. After stating a devastating fact. Maximum 2× per video.Forces the viewer to process what they just heard. Creates weight and gravity.
The golden rule of archival visuals: Every image should change every 5–8 seconds. If any single image stays on screen longer than 10 seconds, viewers start to lose attention. With Ken Burns movement, even a 7-second image feels dynamic. A 15-minute video needs approximately 120–180 individual image placements on the timeline.
MAPS

Map Creation System — Canva (Free)

Maps are essential for military history — they show troop movements, battle locations, and strategic context. Here's how to create professional-looking maps in Canva for free.
StepActionDetails
1Get base mapDownload a clean base map from Wikimedia Commons or David Rumsey. Search "[region] map" or "[country] 1940s map". Choose simple, uncluttered maps. Outline/political maps work better than photographic satellite maps.
2Import to CanvaCreate a 1920×1080 design in Canva. Upload your base map as background. Resize to fill the canvas.
3Add dark overlayAdd a semi-transparent black rectangle over the map (opacity 40–60%). This darkens the map to match your brand palette and makes text/arrows readable.
4Add arrows for movementUse Canva's arrow elements. Color: #DC2626 (Alert Red) for enemy movement, #D97706 (Amber) for allied/Indian movement. Weight: thick enough to see at 720p.
5Add location labelsFont: Oswald Bold, white (#FAFAF9), 14–18pt. Place city/battle names with small circles as markers. Add dates in Special Elite font.
6Add legend (optional)Bottom-left corner: small legend showing what arrow colors represent. Font: JetBrains Mono, 10pt, Foxhole Grey.
7Export as PNGExport at highest quality. Use Ken Burns in CapCut to slowly pan across the map during narration.
Map types you'll create regularly:
Map TypeWhen to UseExample
Theater overviewContext section. Show where in the world this story takes place.Map of Southeast Asia with India, Burma, and Japan highlighted.
Troop movementRising action. Show advances, retreats, flanking maneuvers.Japanese advance toward Imphal with red arrows. Indian defensive positions in amber.
Battle detailClimax. Zoom into the specific battlefield.Close-up of Imphal/Kohima area with specific unit positions marked.
Before/AfterShow territorial changes. Two versions of same map.Map of Europe in 1939 vs. 1945. Or India pre/post Partition.
Pro tip: Create animated maps. In CapCut, place 3–4 versions of the same map (each with arrows at different stages) sequentially on the timeline with crossfade transitions. This simulates an animated troop movement map — the same technique Kings and Generals uses, but done with static Canva images instead of animation software.
TEXT

Text Overlay Templates — On-Screen Information

Text overlays add information without interrupting narration. Create these as reusable presets in CapCut.
Overlay TypeDesignWhen to UseDuration
Stat CardLarge number in Oswald Bold (#FAFAF9), subtitle in Outfit (#A8A29E). Centered. Dark semi-transparent background strip. Example: "87,000" with "Indian soldiers killed in WW2" below.When stating a powerful statistic. Numbers hit harder visually than just hearing them.3–4 seconds
Location/DateBottom-left "lower third". Location in Oswald Bold 16pt, date in Special Elite 12pt below. Amber underline. Example: "IMPHAL, INDIA" / "March 8, 1944"Every time the story moves to a new location or significant date. Orients the viewer.4–5 seconds
Person IDBottom-left lower third. Name in Oswald Bold white, title/rank in Outfit grey below. Example: "GENERAL WILLIAM SLIM" / "Commander, Fourteenth Army"First time a significant person is mentioned. Only for key figures (3–5 per video max).4 seconds
Quote CardFull-screen dark overlay. Quote in Libre Baskerville Italic, amber. Attribution below in Outfit grey. Thin amber border frame.Powerful historical quotes. Maximum 1–2 per video. Must be genuinely impactful.5–6 seconds
"Classified" StampSpecial Elite font, amber (#D97706), angled 15°, slightly transparent. "CLASSIFIED" or "DECLASSIFIED" or "TOP SECRET".Espionage/intelligence videos only. When revealing previously classified information.2–3 seconds (appears and fades)
Save as CapCut presets: Create each overlay type once in CapCut with exact fonts, colors, and animations. Save them as presets or templates. For every new video, you'll just change the text content — the styling stays consistent. This saves 10–15 minutes per video.
EDITING

CapCut Editing Workflow — Step by Step

This is the exact 60-minute editing workflow from Y1.4, now expanded with visual production details.
StepActionTimeDetails
1. Project SetupNew project → 1920×1080 16:9 → Import all assets3 minImport: voiceover WAV, all images (renamed in sequence order), Canva text cards, maps, music track. Organize into folders within the project.
2. Audio FirstPlace voiceover on audio Track 12 minThis is your MASTER timeline. Everything else syncs to the voice. Never move the voiceover once placed.
3. Music LayerPlace background music on Track 23 minVolume at 10–15% of voiceover. Search YouTube Audio Library for "dark cinematic" or "suspense documentary". One continuous track is fine — no need to change music mid-video unless the mood drastically shifts.
4. Image PlacementPlace images on video track, synced to narration25 minFollow your script's [VISUAL CUE] brackets. Each image = 5–8 seconds. Apply Ken Burns to EVERY image (set keyframes for slow zoom or pan). This is the most time-consuming step — batch process by placing all images first, then adding Ken Burns in a second pass.
5. Maps & CardsInsert Canva maps and stat cards at relevant moments8 minPlace maps during strategic/geographic narration. Place stat cards when numbers are mentioned. Place quote cards during powerful quotes.
6. Text OverlaysAdd lower thirds (location/date, person IDs)8 minAdd location/date lower thirds every time the story shifts geography or time. Add person ID the first time each key figure is mentioned.
7. TransitionsAdd crossfade transitions between images5 minStandard crossfade: 0.3–0.5 seconds. Use between images within the same section. Use a brief black screen (1 second) between major sections. Never use fancy transitions (swipes, spins) — they undermine the documentary tone.
8. Title + OutroAdd 3-sec title card after cold open + 15-sec outro3 minTitle: Logo flash with dramatic sting sound. Outro: logo + "Subscribe" text + end screen placeholder cards (2 video slots + subscribe button).
9. ReviewFull playback at 1× speed15 minWatch the entire video. Check: audio levels consistent? Images synced to narration? Text readable? Any jarring transitions? Music not overpowering voice? Fix issues in real-time.
10. ExportExport final video—Settings: 1080p, 30fps, H.264 codec. File size: ~500MB–1.5GB for 15 min. Wait for render to complete before closing.

PACING

Visual Pacing Rules — Never Lose a Viewer

RuleStandardWhy
Image duration (long-form)5–8 seconds per image. Never exceed 10 seconds on a single image.YouTube retention data shows viewer attention drops after 8 seconds of a static visual, even with Ken Burns movement.
Image duration (Shorts)2–3 seconds per image.Shorts viewers swipe in under 2 seconds if nothing changes.
Variety ratioFor every 3 archival photos, include 1 map, text card, or different visual type.Monotonous visual types (photo after photo) cause drop-off. Variety resets attention.
Black screenMaximum 2 per video. 1.5–2 seconds each.Powerful but overuse kills pacing. Reserve for the biggest emotional moments only.
Text on screenMaximum 10 words per text overlay. Stat cards: number + 5-word label.Viewers can't read AND listen simultaneously. Keep text short enough to absorb in 2 seconds.
Music shifts0–1 music changes per video. Use volume changes instead of track changes.Changing music tracks is jarring. Instead: lower music volume during intense narration, raise during visual-only moments.
Transition styleCrossfade ONLY. 0.3–0.5 seconds. Never use star wipes, spins, or novelty transitions.Fancy transitions scream "amateur." Professional documentaries use only cuts and crossfades.

Pre-Edit Asset Preparation (Do Before Opening CapCut)

Spending 10 minutes organizing BEFORE editing saves 20+ minutes of hunting for files DURING editing.
StepActionTime
1Create video folder: TheForgottenFront/Assets/TFF-[number]-[short-title]/1 min
2Download all images from archival sources. Save to the video folder.8 min (during research phase)
3Rename images in sequence order: 01-context-map.jpg, 02-soldiers-marching.jpg, 03-general-slim.jpg, etc. Number matches script order.3 min
4Create Canva assets: All maps, stat cards, quote cards, title card. Export as PNG to the same folder.15 min (during visual assembly phase)
5Download music track from YouTube Audio Library. Save to folder.2 min
6Verify voiceover WAV is in the folder and properly cleaned (Audacity workflow from Y1.4).1 min
When you open CapCut, EVERYTHING should already be in one folder, named in order, ready to drag onto the timeline. This "mise en place" approach (like a chef prepping ingredients before cooking) is the difference between a 60-minute edit session and a 3-hour frustration session.
QC

Visual Quality Checklist (Before Export)

CheckWhat to Verify
☐ Ken Burns on all imagesEvery archival photo has slow zoom or pan. No static images anywhere.
☐ Image pacingNo image stays on screen longer than 10 seconds. Spot check 3 random points in the timeline.
☐ Text readabilityPause on every text overlay. Is it readable at 720p on a phone? If not, increase font size or simplify.
☐ Audio/visual syncWhen narration says "Imphal", the map or photo of Imphal should be on screen — not 5 seconds later.
☐ Music levelsVoice is always clearly audible over music. Music at 10–15% of voice volume. Check at quiet and loud moments.
☐ Color consistencyAll images feel like they belong together. No jarring bright/saturated photo between desaturated ones.
☐ TransitionsOnly crossfades used. No fancy transitions. 0.3–0.5 second duration.
☐ Brand elementsTitle card after cold open (3 sec). Outro with sign-off (15 sec). Colors match palette.
☐ Copyright safetyAll images from approved sources (the 10 listed above). No Google-searched random photos. No AI-generated images.
☐ Demonetization screenNo swastika imagery visible. No graphic violence/casualties. No disturbing close-ups. Review against Gap Analysis Red Zone list.
